Understanding disk-based dynamical mass measurements

By modeling their rotation, circumstellar disks can be used to dynamically "weigh" their host stars. Resolved spectral imaging, obtained using interferometers like the Submillimeter Array and ALMA, reveals the kinematic fingerprint imparted by the rotation of the disk. The characteristics of this fingerprint are a strong function of the central stellar mass. By fitting a model of the disk structure to the interferometric data, we can derive precise constraints on disk size, inclination, and--most importantly--the stellar mass.

Disk geometry and rotation

Astronomers typically model protoplanetary disks with an axisymmetric disk with radial and vertical structure defined by a power law surface density profile and hydrostatic equilibrium. As we will see, how the disk is oriented relative to us has a profound effect on the channel maps we observe. We define the inclination of the disk as the angle between the disk angular momentum axis and the observer. 0 degrees inclination means the disk is viewed face-on (appearing on the sky as a circle), while 90 degrees inclination means the disk is viewed edge-on (appearing on the sky as a thin line).

We can observe the disk in several ways. The first, is by resolving the continuum emission from the dust grains. Another is by measuring the spatially-integrated spectral line emission, or spectrum. The third and most powerful way is to obtain a spatially and spectral resolved image of the disk at the same frequency of a strong spectral line, called channel maps.
